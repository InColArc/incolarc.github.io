Institute for Collaboration Architecture

Research, methodology, and professional development for coordination infrastructure design in knowledge-working organisations.

gGmbH · Rostock, Germany

The Research Question
How should organisations design the coordination infrastructure between their tools and their people — and why has no professional discipline held this mandate for the past sixty-five years?

Knowledge workers spend the majority of their week on coordination that was never designed. Organisations deploy tools, restructure teams, and buy platforms — but the infrastructure layer between governance and operational work remains undesigned. Every established framework — Enterprise Architecture, ITIL, Organisational Development — reaches this boundary and stops.

The Institute studies this gap, develops methodology to address it, and trains practitioners to do the work.

Intellectual Genealogy

The discipline stands on sixty-five years of research that never transferred to practice.

1951
Sociotechnical Systems — Tavistock Institute establishes that technical and social systems must be designed together. The principle was identified. It was not operationalised.
1959
Knowledge Work Theory — Peter Drucker describes what knowledge work requires: autonomy, self-management, fundamentally different organisational structures. Organisations heard "buy computers."
1984
CSCW — Computer-Supported Cooperative Work. Four decades of research into how people collaborate and what technology supports. Theory and methods for support — not for infrastructure design.
1999
Infrastructure Studies — Susan Leigh Star develops methods for making invisible infrastructure visible through breakdown analysis. Star shows how to study infrastructure. The question of how to design it remained open.
2006–
The Practice Branch — Enterprise Social Networks, Working Out Loud, Digital Workplace. Tools were deployed at scale. Nobody designed the coordination structure around them.
2026
Collaboration Architecture — The professional practice of designing coordination infrastructure. The operational discipline that bridges the research-practice gap.

The full argument is developed in the position paper, including the diagnosis of how responsibility for coordination currently falls between Organisational Development and IT Management, and why neither can close the gap alone.

Research Areas

What we study

The Institute develops frameworks and methods grounded in three years of embedded fieldwork in an 11,000-person industrial organisation, in dialogue with CSCW research, Science and Technology Studies, and Enterprise Architecture practice.

KostenAbwälzung

Cost Externalization

How undesigned coordination infrastructure transfers costs systematically to those who cannot refuse them. Developing measurement methods based on hours and euros rather than satisfaction surveys.

Mündigkeit

Organisational Capacity

The conditions under which organisations develop the capacity to design and maintain their own coordination infrastructure — without permanent external dependency. The ethical foundation of the discipline.

Trampelpfade des Wissens

Desire Paths of Knowledge

Archaeological methods for mapping how information actually flows in organisations, making invisible coordination infrastructure visible as a precondition for deliberate design.

Fachwerkhaus-Modell

Timber-Frame Model

A structural model for minimum viable collaborative infrastructure — identifying functional categories of coordination tools and the cross-braces that connect them into a load-bearing whole.

Training & Certification

Building the profession

Germany has almost no Business Architect roles — while the need for coordination infrastructure design grows with every tool deployment, every AI implementation, every organisational restructure. The Institute is developing training and certification to build the profession from the ground up.

Business Architecture & Collaboration Architecture

Training in established Business Architecture frameworks (capability modelling, value stream mapping, strategic alignment) — combined, on request, with Collaboration Architecture qualification: the operational design layer that existing frameworks explicitly scope out.

The certification programme is portfolio-based, requiring demonstrated competence in coordination infrastructure analysis and design — not multiple-choice exams on memorised standards.

Target participants include Enterprise Architects, organisational developers, internal consultants, service designers, and project managers who encounter coordination problems their current frameworks cannot address.
