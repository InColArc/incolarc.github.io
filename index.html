The Problem
Knowledge workers spend 70% of their week on coordination that should have been designed away. Organisations deploy tools. They restructure teams. They buy platforms. But nobody designs the infrastructure between the tools and the people.

Every established framework — Enterprise Architecture, ITIL, Organisational Development — reaches the boundary of coordination infrastructure and stops. Each claims adjacent territory. None occupies the operational layer where coordination actually happens.

The consequence has a name: KostenAbwälzung. When coordination infrastructure is not designed, the cost of making things work lands on whoever cannot refuse it — teams, families, frontline staff. The relevant question is not "how do people feel about collaboration?" but how many hours per week are people spending on coordination work that should have been designed away?

Intellectual Genealogy

Sixty-five years of diagnosis.
No operational discipline — until now.

Collaboration Architecture does not emerge from nowhere. It stands at the intersection of four bodies of knowledge, each of which identified part of the problem and stopped short of solving it.

1959
Knowledge Work Theory — Peter Drucker describes what knowledge work requires: autonomy, self-management, different structures. Organisations heard "buy computers."
1951
Sociotechnical Systems — Tavistock Institute establishes that technical and social systems must be designed together. The principle was identified. It was not operationalised.
1984
CSCW — Computer-Supported Cooperative Work. Four decades of research into how people collaborate and what technology supports. Theory and methods for support — not for infrastructure design.
1999
Infrastructure Studies — Susan Leigh Star develops methods for making invisible infrastructure visible. Star shows how to study infrastructure. The question of how to design it remained open.
2006–
The Practice Branch — Enterprise Social Networks, Working Out Loud, Digital Workplace. Everyone sold tools. Nobody designed structure.
2026
Collaboration Architecture — The professional practice of designing coordination infrastructure. The operational discipline that bridges the research-practice gap.
Key Frameworks

Instruments, not abstractions

These frameworks are validated through three years of embedded practice in an 11,000-person industrial organisation, not derived from theory alone.

KostenAbwälzung

Cost Externalization

When coordination infrastructure is absent, costs transfer systematically to those who cannot refuse them. Measuring this — in hours, not satisfaction surveys — reveals the true cost of undesigned infrastructure.

Mündigkeit

Organisational Capacity

The capacity for self-determined action. Build capability, not dependency. Transfer knowledge, don't hoard it. The ethical foundation of the discipline: organisations that understand their own coordination infrastructure can maintain it without permanent external help.

Trampelpfade des Wissens

Desire Paths of Knowledge

Mapping how information actually flows — versus how org charts suggest it should. The archaeological method that makes invisible coordination infrastructure visible before redesigning it.

Fachwerkhaus-Modell

Timber-Frame Model

A minimum viable collaborative structure — visualising the functional categories of coordination tools and the cross-braces that hold them together. The mnemonic device that makes infrastructure design tangible.
